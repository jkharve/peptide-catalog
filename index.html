Understand what you're putting in your body — and why.

Peptides are small proteins your body either makes naturally or can be supplemented with. This guide breaks down the most discussed peptides in plain language — what they do, how they're used, what to watch for, and what questions to ask your doctor.

BPC-157
Body Protection Compound
Recovery

BPC-157 is derived from a protein found naturally in gastric juice. It's one of the most talked-about healing peptides because of its remarkable tissue repair effects — particularly on the gut, tendons, and ligaments. It's widely known as "the healing peptide" and praised for its accessibility even for beginners.

Primary Uses
  • Gut healing — leaky gut, IBS, Crohn's
  • Tendon & ligament repair
  • Reducing inflammation systemically
  • Joint pain relief
  • Accelerating post-surgery recovery
Administration
  • Injectable (subcutaneous) OR oral capsules
  • Oral works best for gut issues; injectable for systemic/joint repair
  • Dose: 250–500 mcg/day
  • Cycle: 4–8 weeks on
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Generally very well tolerated
  • Nausea or lightheadedness if dose is too high
  • Possible vascular growth (pro-angiogenic) — caution with existing tumors
  • Not enough long-term human data yet
  • Injection site irritation possible
Women's Notes
  • One of the safest options for women to explore
  • Great for post-childbirth pelvic/connective tissue repair
  • No hormonal interference reported
  • Particularly popular among women with gut issues
TB-500
Thymosin Beta-4 (synthetic fragment)
Recovery

TB-500 is a synthetic version of a portion of Thymosin Beta-4, a protein present in nearly every cell in the body. It's often used alongside BPC-157 for an enhanced healing stack. It excels specifically at muscle repair and flexibility improvements.

Primary Uses
  • Muscle tissue repair after injury
  • Improved flexibility and range of motion
  • Reduces inflammation in injured tissue
  • Hair regrowth (emerging research)
  • Wound healing acceleration
Administration
  • Injectable only (subcutaneous or intramuscular)
  • Dose: 2–5 mg, twice per week
  • Loading phase: 2–2.5 mg twice per week × 4–6 weeks
  • Maintenance: 2–2.5 mg once per week
  • Cycle: 4–6 weeks on
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Head rush/lightheadedness shortly after injection
  • Fatigue during loading phase
  • Pro-angiogenic — same tumor caution as BPC-157
  • Not recommended during pregnancy
  • Nausea possible at higher doses
Women's Notes
  • Popular with women athletes for injury recovery
  • The BPC-157 + TB-500 combo ("wolverine stack") is widely used
  • Hair regrowth potential being studied
  • No significant hormonal disruption noted
Semaglutide
GLP-1 Agonist · Ozempic / Wegovy
Weight

Perhaps the most discussed peptide of the decade. Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that mimics a natural gut hormone to regulate blood sugar and dramatically reduce appetite. Understanding how to use it responsibly — protecting muscle and knowing its limitations — is essential.

Primary Uses
  • Significant weight loss (10–15%+ body weight in trials)
  • Blood sugar regulation / Type 2 diabetes management
  • Appetite suppression — reduces "food noise"
  • Cardiovascular risk reduction
  • Metabolic syndrome treatment
Administration
  • Weekly subcutaneous injection
  • Start low (0.25 mg/week) and titrate up slowly
  • FDA approved up to 2.4 mg/week for weight loss
  • Also available as oral form (lower bioavailability)
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nausea — most common, usually dose-dependent
  • Vomiting, diarrhea, constipation
  • Muscle loss if protein intake/resistance training neglected
  • Pancreatitis risk (rare but serious)
  • "Ozempic face" — facial fat loss at higher doses
  • Thyroid C-cell tumor risk in rodent studies (theoretical in humans)
  • GERD worsening possible
  • Rebound weight gain if stopped without lifestyle support
Women's Notes
  • Particularly effective for women with PCOS
  • May improve hormonal profiles as body fat reduces
  • Protein intake (1g per lb lean mass) is non-negotiable
  • Resistance training essential to preserve muscle
  • Not for use during pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • May affect fertility — discuss with OB/GYN
Tirzepatide
GIP/GLP-1 Dual Agonist · Mounjaro / Zepbound
Weight

The next evolution after semaglutide. Tirzepatide activates two receptors (GLP-1 and GIP) simultaneously, producing even greater weight loss — averaging 20–22% of body weight in trials. It is a more potent tool that demands even more respect around muscle preservation.

Primary Uses
  • Aggressive weight loss (superior to semaglutide)
  • Type 2 diabetes — excellent glycemic control
  • Improved insulin sensitivity
  • Lipid profile improvement
Administration
  • Weekly subcutaneous injection
  • Start at 2.5 mg/week, titrate monthly
  • Range: 2.5–15 mg/week
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Similar GI side effects to semaglutide but can be more intense
  • Greater muscle loss risk — mandatory resistance training
  • Hypoglycemia risk if combined with other diabetes meds
  • Slower gastric emptying — timing of other medications matters
  • Same thyroid caution as semaglutide
Women's Notes
  • More powerful — not necessarily better as a starting point
  • Women tend to respond very well, but GI effects can be pronounced
  • Bone density monitoring recommended on extended use
  • Not for use during pregnancy
Retatrutide
LY3437943 · Triple-G · GLP-1 / GIP / Glucagon Agonist
Weight

Retatrutide is the next frontier in weight loss pharmacology — a triple-receptor agonist that simultaneously activates GLP-1, GIP, AND glucagon receptors. Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) hits one receptor. Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) hits two. Retatrutide hits all three, and Phase 2 clinical trial data published in the New England Journal of Medicine showed an average of 24% body weight loss in just 48 weeks — the largest average weight reduction ever recorded for an obesity medication in clinical trials. It is currently in Phase 3 trials and not yet FDA approved, with approval potentially expected around 2027. It is available through some compounding pharmacies and clinical trial access.

Primary Uses
  • Aggressive weight loss — up to 24%+ of body weight in trials
  • Appetite suppression (GLP-1 + GIP)
  • Fat burning — glucagon receptor increases energy expenditure
  • Blood sugar regulation and Type 2 diabetes management
  • Improved insulin sensitivity and lipid profiles
  • Waist circumference reduction
  • Cardiovascular risk reduction (TRIUMPH outcomes trial ongoing)
Administration
  • Weekly subcutaneous injection
  • Start at 1–2 mg/week — slower titration than semaglutide or tirzepatide
  • Increase every 4 weeks: 1 → 2 → 4 → 6 → 8 mg
  • Max studied dose: 12 mg/week
  • Titration pace is critical — rushing escalation nearly doubles GI side effects
  • Not yet FDA approved; available through clinical trials or compounding
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation — similar to other GLP-1s but can be more intense
  • Muscle loss — greatest risk of all weight-loss peptides due to speed of loss; resistance training is mandatory
  • Mild heart rate increase at higher doses (generally resolves)
  • Gallbladder issues — risk increases with rapid weight loss
  • Elevated liver enzymes reported in some participants
  • Same thyroid C-cell caution as semaglutide and tirzepatide
  • Pancreatitis (rare but class-wide concern)
  • Long-term safety data beyond 1 year not yet available
  • FDA has flagged counterfeit retatrutide being sold online — sourcing is a serious concern
Women's Notes
  • The most powerful weight loss peptide currently in the pipeline — not a starting point
  • Protein intake and resistance training are even more critical here than with semaglutide/tirzepatide
  • Bone density monitoring is important — rapid weight loss accelerates bone loss in women
  • Hair loss (telogen effluvium) is a known side effect of significant rapid weight loss — typically temporary
  • Not for use during pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Because it is pre-approval, physician oversight and sourcing from legitimate channels is especially important
  • Women with PCOS or insulin resistance may see particularly strong metabolic benefits
CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin
GHRH + GHRP Stack
Growth

The most commonly prescribed peptide combo by anti-aging physicians. CJC-1295 extends the life of growth hormone releasing hormone, while Ipamorelin selectively stimulates a growth hormone pulse — without significantly raising cortisol or appetite (unlike other GHRPs). Together they create sustained GH elevation.

Primary Uses
  • Improved body composition (fat loss + lean muscle)
  • Anti-aging — improved skin, hair, nails
  • Better sleep quality (GH pulses during deep sleep)
  • Faster injury recovery
  • Enhanced energy and vitality
  • IGF-1 elevation
Administration
  • Injectable, typically dosed together as a blend
  • CJC-1295 dose: 100–200 mcg, 1–3x daily
  • Ipamorelin dose: 200–300 mcg, 2–3x daily
  • Common combined dose: 300 mcg CJC + 300 mcg Ipamorelin per injection
  • Administer before bed on an empty stomach; some add a morning dose pre-workout
  • Cycle: 8–12 weeks on
  • Break: 4–6 weeks between cycles
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Water retention, especially early on
  • Tingling/numbness in hands (carpal tunnel-like)
  • Fatigue if GH is elevated too high
  • Can raise blood glucose (GH is counter-regulatory)
  • Not appropriate if active cancer (GH is pro-growth)
  • Potential to suppress natural GH production long-term
Women's Notes
  • Women are more sensitive to GH effects — start with lower doses
  • Water retention can be frustrating — usually resolves in weeks 2–4
  • Excellent for women in perimenopause experiencing body composition shifts
  • Can complement estrogen optimization protocols
  • Monitor IGF-1 levels via bloodwork
Tesamorelin
GHRH Analogue · Egrifta / Egrifta WR
Growth

Tesamorelin is a synthetic 44-amino acid analogue of growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) — modified at the N-terminus for improved stability and a longer half-life than natural GHRH. It's the only GHRH analogue with an FDA-approved indication (Egrifta, approved 2010), originally developed for reducing visceral abdominal fat in HIV patients with lipodystrophy. What makes it stand apart from other GH peptides is its laser focus on visceral adipose tissue (VAT) — the deep metabolically dangerous fat around internal organs — while preserving lean muscle. Unlike direct HGH injections, tesamorelin works by triggering your pituitary gland to release GH naturally and in a pulsatile pattern, keeping the body's own hormone feedback intact.

Primary Uses
  • Visceral (belly) fat reduction — the primary, most documented benefit
  • Body composition improvement without muscle loss
  • IGF-1 elevation and GH axis stimulation
  • Metabolic health — improved lipid profiles, reduced liver fat
  • Cardiovascular risk reduction (visceral fat is a major driver)
  • Skin quality and collagen synthesis (via GH/IGF-1 pathway)
  • Recovery, sleep quality, and overall vitality
  • Cognitive support (emerging research in older adults)
Administration
  • Daily subcutaneous injection into the lower abdomen
  • Dose: 1–2 mg once daily
  • Best administered before bed — aligns with natural nighttime GH release
  • Rotate injection sites to prevent tissue irritation
  • Cycle: 8–12 weeks typical; up to 16–20 weeks for persistent visceral fat
  • Break: 4–6 weeks between cycles
  • Lyophilized powder — reconstitute with sterile water before use; refrigerate after
  • IGF-1 labs at baseline and 6–8 weeks into protocol
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Injection site redness, itching, or mild swelling — most common
  • Water retention and mild joint stiffness — especially early on
  • Nausea or stomach discomfort — typically resolves after first 1–2 weeks
  • Increased appetite in some users
  • Glucose sensitivity — GH can interfere with insulin signaling; monitor fasting glucose and HbA1c, especially with insulin resistance or prediabetes
  • Contraindicated with active malignancy or history of pituitary tumors
  • Contraindicated during pregnancy — animal studies showed fetal harm (FDA Category X)
  • Fat may return after discontinuation — results are not permanent without maintenance
Women's Notes
  • Particularly valuable for women in perimenopause/menopause — visceral fat accumulation accelerates with estrogen decline
  • Targets abdominal fat that is notoriously resistant to diet and exercise alone
  • Does not cause the muscle loss seen with GLP-1 agonists — a key advantage
  • Works synergistically with CJC-1295/Ipamorelin for comprehensive GH support
  • Women with PCOS or insulin resistance should monitor blood sugar closely
  • FDA-approved status means more clinical data and prescribing infrastructure than most peptides
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding — FDA Category X
Sermorelin
GHRH Analogue
Growth

Often described as the "gentler" growth hormone stimulator. Sermorelin was one of the first peptides to gain FDA approval (for growth hormone deficiency in children). It's considered safer and more physiologically natural than direct GH injections because it works through the pituitary's normal feedback mechanisms.

Primary Uses
  • Anti-aging and vitality protocols
  • Improved sleep quality
  • Gradual body composition improvement
  • Libido enhancement
  • Cognitive clarity
Administration
  • Injectable subcutaneous, before bed (aligns with natural GH pulse)
  • Dose: 0.2–0.3 mg (200–300 mcg) nightly
  • Cycle: 3–6 months, with potential maintenance dosing thereafter
  • Break: 1–2 months between cycles
  • Results are gradual — noticeable changes typically after 2–3 months
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Injection site redness
  • Headaches (usually temporary)
  • Flushing
  • Water retention (less than direct GH)
  • Very short half-life — requires precise timing
Women's Notes
  • One of the preferred first-entry GH peptides for women
  • Gentler effects make it easier to tolerate
  • Popular in 40s–60s age range for vitality
  • Works best in women who are already optimizing other hormones
PT-141
Bremelanotide · Vyleesi
Libido

The only peptide FDA-approved specifically for female sexual dysfunction. PT-141 works on the brain's melanocortin receptors to increase sexual desire — not through hormones or vascular effects (unlike Viagra), but centrally, through desire itself. It's particularly relevant for women with hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD).

Primary Uses
  • Female sexual desire / HSDD treatment
  • Male sexual dysfunction resistant to PDE5 inhibitors
  • Central (brain-based) arousal enhancement
  • Used by both men and women for enhanced intimacy
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection 45 min–2 hrs before activity
  • Dose: 1–2 mg as needed (start very low at 0.5 mg)
  • Cycle: As-needed use; max 8 times per month
  • Not to be used more than once per 24 hours
  • No established long daily-dosing cycle — strictly event-based use
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nausea — most common, often significant
  • Flushing and warmth (face and body)
  • Transient blood pressure increase
  • Headache
  • Not for use with cardiovascular disease without physician clearance
  • Spontaneous erections in men at higher doses
  • Hyperpigmentation with frequent use
Women's Notes
  • FDA-approved for women — Vyleesi is the prescription auto-injector
  • Works even without hormonal optimization (unlike many other options)
  • Premedication with anti-nausea meds can significantly help
  • Particularly useful postpartum or during perimenopause
  • Effects felt centrally — increased desire/arousal, not just physical
Epithalon
Epitalon · Tetrapeptide
Longevity

Epithalon is a synthetic tetrapeptide considered one of the most promising anti-aging peptides. It works by activating telomerase — the enzyme responsible for maintaining telomere length, which is directly linked to cellular aging. It's been studied extensively in Russia for decades. It is discussed extensively in true longevity protocols.

Primary Uses
  • Telomere extension (cellular aging)
  • Improved sleep regulation via melatonin normalization
  • Immune system modulation
  • Antioxidant properties
  • Possible cancer prevention (animal studies)
Administration
  • Injectable (subcutaneous) or intranasal; oral has very low bioavailability
  • Dose: 5–10 mg/day subcutaneously
  • Cycle: 10–20 days per course
  • Frequency: Repeat 2–4 times per year
  • Break: 2–3 months between cycles
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Generally very well tolerated in studies
  • Theoretically, telomerase activation could support cancer cell survival
  • Limited long-term human clinical data
  • Injection site reactions possible
  • Vivid dreams noted by some users
Women's Notes
  • Popular in anti-aging circles for women 40+
  • Sleep normalization effects particularly valued
  • Often combined with other longevity protocols
  • No significant hormonal effects reported
DSIP
Delta Sleep-Inducing Peptide
Sleep

DSIP is a naturally occurring peptide that appears in the brain and peripheral organs. As the name suggests, it has pronounced effects on sleep architecture — particularly increasing deep, slow-wave sleep, which is where the most recovery and GH release occurs.

Primary Uses
  • Sleep quality improvement
  • Reduction of stress and anxiety
  • Insomnia relief
  • Stress hormone (cortisol) modulation
  • GH release stimulation during sleep
Administration
  • Injectable subcutaneous before bed
  • 100–300 mcg typical dose
  • Not meant for nightly indefinite use
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Paradoxical wakefulness in some users
  • Headache
  • Less studied than other peptides
  • Variable individual response
Women's Notes
  • Appealing for women with sleep disruptions from hormonal shifts
  • May complement GH peptide protocols (synergistic sleep benefits)
  • Individual response varies widely — start low
GHK-Cu
Copper Peptide
Beauty

GHK-Cu (Glycine-Histidine-Lysine complexed with copper) is a naturally occurring human plasma peptide that declines dramatically with age. It has extensive research behind its regenerative properties — in skin, hair, and wound healing. It's used systemically via injection for comprehensive regenerative benefits.

Primary Uses
  • Skin rejuvenation — collagen, elastin stimulation
  • Hair follicle stimulation and growth
  • Wound healing
  • Anti-inflammatory systemically
  • DNA repair and antioxidant activity
Administration
  • Injectable subcutaneous only (injectable delivers systemic benefits)
  • Dose: 1–2 mg/day (cheat sheet); 2–4 mg 2–3x per week (clinical protocols)
  • Cycle: 4–6 weeks on
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Very well-tolerated — naturally occurring compound
  • Copper accumulation with very high doses (theoretical)
  • Injection site mild irritation possible
Women's Notes
  • Highly popular for skin, hair, and connective tissue goals
  • Combines well with other beauty and longevity protocols
  • Injectable form delivers systemic benefits throughout the body
Semax
ACTH(4-7)PGP · Nasal Spray Nootropic
Neuro

Semax is a synthetic peptide developed in Russia in the 1980s, derived from a fragment of adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH). It's been a registered pharmaceutical in Russia for decades, used clinically for stroke recovery, cognitive disorders, and optic nerve disease. What makes it unique is its nasal spray delivery — it passes directly through the nasal mucosa and reaches the brain within minutes, bypassing digestion entirely. It's become one of the most popular nootropic peptides for focus, mental clarity, and neuroprotection.

Primary Uses
  • Cognitive enhancement — focus, memory, attention
  • Neuroprotection and brain cell survival
  • BDNF and NGF elevation (brain growth factors)
  • Mood stabilization — serotonin and dopamine modulation
  • Mental fatigue and stress resilience
  • Stroke recovery and neuroregeneration (clinical use in Russia)
  • Optic nerve support
Administration
  • Nasal spray — primary and most studied route
  • 0.1% solution for cognitive enhancement (most common)
  • Dose: 300–600 mcg/day divided AM/PM; some protocols up to 1 mg/day
  • 1–2 drops per nostril, 1–2x daily in the morning only
  • Effects felt within 15–30 minutes, lasting up to 24 hours
  • Cycle: 4–6 weeks continuous use (or shorter 5–14 day intensive cycles)
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
  • Do not use close to bedtime — stimulating effect may disrupt sleep
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nasal irritation, burning sensation — most common
  • Headache — usually temporary, often dose-related
  • Insomnia or restlessness if taken too late in the day
  • Mild increased anxiety in some users
  • Nausea or dizziness at higher doses
  • May slightly raise blood glucose in diabetics (~7% of patients in studies)
  • Caution with severe psychiatric disorders — may exacerbate anxiety or mania
  • Not FDA approved; limited long-term human data
Women's Notes
  • Popular among women dealing with brain fog, perimenopause-related cognitive decline
  • Nasal delivery is needle-free — an easy entry into peptide use
  • Morning use only — prevents sleep disruption
  • Start with 0.1% solution before considering higher concentrations
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding (no safety data)
  • N-Acetyl Semax Amidate is a modified version with potentially greater potency
Selank
TP-7 · Anxiolytic Nasal Spray
Neuro

Selank is often described as Semax's calmer sibling. Also developed in Russia, it's a synthetic analogue of tuftsin — a naturally occurring immune peptide. Where Semax energizes and focuses, Selank calms and stabilizes. It's been studied as an anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) compound that works like a benzodiazepine in terms of calming effects — but without the sedation, memory impairment, or addiction potential that makes benzos problematic. It's available as a nasal spray and can be prescribed through US compounding pharmacies.

Primary Uses
  • Anxiety and generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
  • PTSD and stress-related disorders
  • Depression — especially anxiety-driven
  • ADHD-related attention and cognitive difficulties
  • BDNF elevation and neuroprotection
  • Immune modulation and antiviral properties
  • Cognitive stability — memory and learning
Administration
  • Nasal spray (preferred) — rapid CNS absorption in under 10 minutes
  • Also available as subcutaneous injection
  • Dose: 300–600 mcg/day divided AM/PM; some protocols up to 1 mg/day in high-stress periods
  • Nasal: 300–900 mcg, 2–3x daily; Injectable: 0.5 mg once or twice daily
  • Cycle: 4–6 weeks continuous use
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
  • Can be taken with food — no fasting required
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nasal/throat irritation from spray
  • Mild headache
  • Slight drowsiness in some users (mild)
  • Rare dizziness
  • May interact with CNS medications (SSRIs, benzodiazepines) — physician guidance required
  • Limited English-language long-term safety data
  • Not FDA approved for therapeutic use
Women's Notes
  • Highly relevant for women dealing with hormonal anxiety — perimenopause, postpartum
  • A compelling alternative to SSRIs or benzos for situational/chronic anxiety
  • No sexual side effects (unlike most antidepressants)
  • No dependency or withdrawal — significant advantage over benzos
  • Available via compounding pharmacy with a prescription
  • Can be used alongside SSRIs — may reduce side effects (discuss with physician)
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding
Oxytocin
The "Love Hormone" · 9-Amino Acid Neuropeptide
Neuro

Oxytocin is a naturally occurring 9-amino acid peptide hormone produced in the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary. Best known for its roles in labor, breastfeeding, and mother-infant bonding, it's now being explored far beyond obstetrics — as a tool for reducing anxiety and stress, improving emotional connection, supporting sexual function, and even metabolic health. What makes oxytocin unique is that it straddles the line between hormone and neuropeptide: it acts both peripherally (on the body) and centrally (in the brain), and its effects in women are particularly well-supported by research. Intranasal delivery is the preferred route for mood and cognitive effects because it bypasses the blood-brain barrier and reaches central receptors more directly than IV administration.

Primary Uses
  • Anxiety and stress reduction — lowers cortisol, promotes calm
  • Social bonding, trust, and emotional connection enhancement
  • Female sexual function — arousal, sensitivity, satisfaction
  • Postpartum bonding and mood support
  • Autism spectrum — improving social responsiveness (active research area)
  • Relationship satisfaction and emotional intimacy
  • Appetite and metabolic support — emerging research
  • Recovery from addiction and substance dependence (early studies)
Administration
  • Intranasal spray — preferred for mood, social, and cognitive effects; acts within minutes, lasts ~1 hour
  • Subcutaneous injection — preferred for peripheral/body effects
  • Troche (sublingual) and topical/vaginal gel also available via compounding pharmacy
  • SubQ dose: 20–80 mcg, 30–45 min before social interaction or intimacy
  • Intranasal dose: 24–40 IU (clinical research range)
  • Cycle: No established long-term daily schedule — use as-needed or short contextual protocols
  • Compounded formulations require a prescription; not FDA-approved for mood/bonding indications
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Headache and nasal irritation — most common with intranasal use
  • Transient drowsiness or fatigue, especially with higher doses
  • Mild nausea or dizziness occasionally reported
  • Mood shifts — some feel calmer, others may experience irritability or emotional blunting
  • Heightened emotional sensitivity — can amplify negative feelings as well as positive ones
  • Cortisol suppression: beneficial as a goal but excessive suppression can impair adaptive stress response
  • May uterine contractions — avoid during pregnancy
  • Caution with borderline personality disorder — can sometimes increase suspicion or reactivity
Women's Notes
  • Women show especially strong responses — research confirms oxytocin enhances salience of positive social cues in women specifically
  • Highly relevant for female sexual dysfunction — directly studied for arousal and satisfaction
  • Pairs well with PT-141 for sexual function; pairs well with Selank for anxiety reduction
  • Perimenopause/menopause: oxytocin levels decline with estrogen — supplementing may address emotional disconnection, low libido, and social withdrawal
  • Postpartum mood: may support bonding and reduce postpartum anxiety (with physician oversight)
  • Do not use during pregnancy — oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions
  • Avoid breastfeeding use unless specifically directed by a provider
Pinealon
EDR Peptide · Glu-Asp-Arg · Khavinson Bioregulator
Neuro

Pinealon is a synthetic tripeptide (Glu-Asp-Arg) developed at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology — the same Russian research group behind Epithalon. It belongs to the Khavinson family of peptide bioregulators, compounds designed to influence gene expression and slow aspects of cellular aging. Where Epithalon targets systemic longevity and telomeres, Pinealon is specifically targeted at the central nervous system and the pineal gland — the brain's circadian master clock. What makes Pinealon mechanistically unusual is that its small tripeptide structure may allow it to cross both cell membranes and nuclear membranes, potentially interacting directly with DNA rather than through surface receptors. Most research is Russian-origin with limited Western replication, but clinical evidence and its safety profile are both favorable.

Primary Uses
  • Sleep quality improvement and circadian rhythm restoration
  • Melatonin production optimization via pineal gland support
  • Cognitive enhancement — memory, focus, processing speed
  • Neuroprotection against age-related neurodegeneration
  • Serotonin synthesis support in cortical neurons
  • Irisin expression — linked to telomere protection and anti-aging
  • Recovery from brain injury or cognitive burnout
  • Oxidative stress reduction in neural tissue
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection: 100–300 mcg once daily
  • Also available as oral supplement (lower bioavailability than SubQ)
  • Typical cycle: 10–20 days on, then off for several months before repeating
  • Cycles often repeated seasonally (2–3x per year)
  • Often stacked with Semax (cognitive stimulation) or Selank (calm focus)
  • Not FDA-approved; primarily available as research compound or via specialty compounding
  • Source quality critical — purchase only from verified, tested suppliers
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Generally very well-tolerated — one of the safest peptides in the Khavinson family
  • Mild injection site reactions possible
  • Occasional drowsiness — consistent with its circadian/melatonin effects; take in evenings
  • Mild headache reported in some users, typically transient
  • Long-term safety data from clinical use rather than large controlled trials
  • Most research conducted in Russian institutions with limited independent replication
  • No FDA approval; safety in pregnancy and breastfeeding not established — avoid
Women's Notes
  • Particularly relevant for perimenopausal and menopausal women — sleep disruption and circadian dysfunction are hallmark symptoms as estrogen declines
  • Supports melatonin and serotonin pathways that tend to decline with age and hormonal changes
  • Non-hormonal — no interference with the HPO axis or HRT protocols
  • Stacks well with Semax + Selank for a comprehensive neuro support protocol (cognitive clarity + calm + sleep)
  • Pairs naturally with Epithalon for a longevity-focused anti-aging stack
  • Low-risk profile makes it accessible as a starting point for neuro peptide therapy
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding — safety not established
Melanotan 2
"Barbie Drug" · Non-Selective MC Agonist
Beauty

Melanotan 2 (MT2) is the more potent, broader-acting sibling of MT1. It activates multiple melanocortin receptors (MC1R, MC3R, MC4R, MC5R) — producing faster and deeper tanning, but also appetite suppression, increased libido, and other systemic effects that MT1 doesn't significantly produce. PT-141 (bremelanotide, the FDA-approved female libido peptide) is actually a metabolite of MT2. It has no regulatory approval and is widely available as a research compound, which means quality control is a significant concern.

Primary Uses
  • Rapid, deep skin tanning with minimal UV
  • Appetite suppression and potential fat loss (MC4R activation)
  • Libido enhancement — via MC4R in the CNS
  • Improved insulin sensitivity and glucose regulation (emerging research)
  • Anti-inflammatory properties
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection only
  • Dose: 0.25–1 mg every other day
  • Loading dose: 250–500 mcg daily until desired tan achieved
  • Maintenance: 500 mcg once or twice weekly
  • Cycle: 2–3 months
  • Break: 4–8 weeks between cycles
  • Some UV exposure recommended to activate melanin production
  • Keep reconstituted vials refrigerated; use within 4 weeks
Side Effects & Cautions

NAD+
Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide · Cellular Coenzyme
Longevity

NAD+ is not a traditional peptide — it's a coenzyme found in every cell in your body, central to how cells produce energy, repair DNA, and communicate. The reason it appears in peptide therapy discussions is that NAD+ levels decline by roughly 30% between ages 45–60, and this decline accelerates through perimenopause. Injectable NAD+ bypasses the digestive system for direct cellular delivery. It's one of the most discussed anti-aging and energy restoration compounds in functional medicine, and it pairs particularly well with traditional peptide protocols.

Primary Uses
  • Cellular energy production (mitochondrial function / ATP)
  • DNA repair and genomic integrity
  • Anti-aging — reducing age-related cellular decline
  • Brain fog and cognitive clarity
  • Metabolic support — insulin sensitivity, weight management
  • Post-illness recovery (long COVID, burnout, fatigue)
  • Addiction recovery — reducing cravings and withdrawal symptoms
  • Improved sleep regulation and mood
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection (most convenient, at-home friendly)
  • Intramuscular injection
  • IV infusion (fastest delivery; requires clinical setting)
  • Oral precursors: NMN, NR, NADH (lower bioavailability than injectable)
  • Oral dose: 100–500 mg/day
  • SubQ dose: 20–100 mg per injection (lower end vs IV)
  • IV/IM dose: 100–300 mg per session
  • Cycle: 4–12 weeks, with potential ongoing maintenance dosing
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles

Side Effects & Cautions
  • Nausea (rare with SubQ; more common with IV at high rates)
  • Flushing and warmth during administration
  • Injection site tenderness
  • Mild headache or lightheadedness after first sessions
  • Temporary fatigue as body adjusts (cellular-level shifts)
  • Transient mood sensitivity in some patients
  • Very high doses may reduce insulin sensitivity — caution with diabetes
  • Potential liver strain at excessive doses
  • NAD+ works at the cellular level — results are gradual, not immediate
Women's Notes
  • Particularly relevant for perimenopause — NAD+ decline mirrors hormonal decline timing
  • Addresses the fatigue, brain fog, and metabolic slowdown of midlife from the cellular level
  • Pairs well with HRT — complementary rather than competing
  • Oral precursors (NMN, NR) are an accessible starting point without injections
  • Injectable NAD+ shows faster and more pronounced results vs oral
  • Results build over weeks — patience is essential
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding
MOTS-c
Mitochondrial-Derived Peptide · Metabolic Regulator
Longevity

MOTS-c is one of the most scientifically fascinating peptides in this catalog — it's encoded not by nuclear DNA like most peptides, but by mitochondrial DNA. It was only discovered in 2015 and represents a new class of mitochondrial-derived peptides (MDPs). The body naturally produces MOTS-c in response to exercise and metabolic stress, but levels decline with age. It functions as a "mitokine" — a hormone-like signal from the mitochondria that optimizes how the entire body manages energy. Think of it as the body's way of mimicking the cellular benefits of exercise and caloric restriction at the molecular level.

Primary Uses
  • Metabolic health — insulin sensitivity and glucose regulation
  • Fat oxidation and body composition support
  • Mitochondrial biogenesis (creating new mitochondria)
  • Anti-aging and longevity — AMPK pathway activation
  • Physical endurance and performance
  • Bone metabolism and density support
  • Anti-inflammatory and immune regulation
  • Cognitive protection (emerging research)
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection only
  • Dose: 5–10 mg every 5 days (cheat sheet); 5–15 mg/week split 2–3x (clinical protocols)
  • Common schedule: Monday, Wednesday, Friday
  • Best timed: morning, fasted, before exercise
  • Cycle: 20 days (cheat sheet); or 2–4 weeks on
  • Break: 2–4 weeks between cycles
  • Beginners start at 5 mg/week and titrate up
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Generally well-tolerated — naturally occurring in humans
  • Injection site irritation possible
  • Mild fatigue during initial AMPK adjustment period
  • Appetite changes (suppression or increase)
  • Rare muscle cramping — possibly electrolyte-related
  • Theoretical hypoglycemia risk when combined with fasting or insulin-sensitizing protocols
  • Mostly preclinical (animal) data — limited long-term human trials
  • Hormonal effects at very high or prolonged doses warrant monitoring
Women's Notes
  • Exciting for women with insulin resistance, PCOS, or metabolic syndrome
  • Bone density support is particularly relevant for women over 40
  • May enhance fat oxidation without sacrificing muscle — unlike GLP-1 agonists alone
  • Pairs naturally with NAD+ for a comprehensive mitochondrial support protocol
  • Morning fasted injection maximizes metabolic signaling effects
  • One of the newer peptides — discuss current evidence with your physician
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding
Thymosin Alpha-1
Tα1 · Thymalfasin · Zadaxin
Longevity

Thymosin Alpha-1 (Tα1) is a 28-amino acid peptide naturally produced by the thymus gland — the organ responsible for training and regulating the immune system. Isolated in the 1970s, it's one of the most clinically studied peptides in existence, with thousands of patients treated across infectious disease, oncology, and immune deficiency research. The synthetic form, thymalfasin (brand name Zadaxin), is approved in over 35 countries for treating hepatitis B and C and is used as immune support in cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy. It works by boosting T-cell production and activation — particularly CD4+ and CD8+ cells — while simultaneously modulating (not just stimulating) the immune response to prevent excessive inflammation. This dual action makes it uniquely valuable: it can help an underactive immune system fight harder and help an overactive immune system calm down.

Primary Uses
  • Immune system strengthening — T-cell activation, NK cell enhancement
  • Chronic viral infections: hepatitis B & C, HIV, Epstein-Barr, long-COVID
  • Cancer support — adjunct to chemotherapy, improved immune response to tumors
  • Autoimmune conditions — modulates rather than simply stimulates (calms overactive immunity)
  • Vaccine adjuvant — enhances immune response to vaccines, especially in elderly or immunocompromised
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome / ME-CFS (emerging evidence)
  • Mold toxicity and chronic inflammatory response syndrome (CIRS)
  • General immune resilience and longevity
Administration
  • Subcutaneous injection — standard route; reconstituted lyophilized powder
  • Standard dose: 1.6 mg subcutaneously, twice weekly
  • General wellness range: 1.0–1.5 mg, 2–3x per week
  • Chronic viral infection protocol: 1.5 mg twice weekly for 6–12 months
  • Acute infection protocol: 1.5 mg daily for 5–10 days
  • Vaccine adjuvant: single 1.6 mg dose on day of vaccination
  • Cycle: 4–12 weeks; can be repeated several times per year
  • Break: 4–8 weeks between cycles
  • Reconstitute with bacteriostatic water; refrigerate; discard after 30 days
  • Prescription required in the US — available via compounding pharmacy
Side Effects & Cautions
  • Extremely well-tolerated — among the best safety profiles of any peptide; 3,000+ patients studied
  • Mild injection site reactions: redness, itching, minor swelling
  • Transient fatigue or flu-like symptoms early in treatment — sign of immune activation
  • Rare: low-grade fever, mild muscle aches, headache
  • Caution with severe autoimmune conditions — immune modulation may occasionally trigger flares in susceptible individuals
  • Do not use in individuals being deliberately immunosuppressed (e.g., organ transplant recipients)
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding unless specifically directed by a physician
Women's Notes
  • Women are disproportionately affected by autoimmune diseases (80% of cases) — Tα1's immune-modulating (not just stimulating) action is ideal
  • Relevant for recurrent infections, chronic fatigue, and post-viral syndromes common in women
  • Thymic function declines with age — supplementing Tα1 can partially compensate for age-related immune decline
  • Pairs well with BPC-157 (for healing + immune support) and NAD+ (for cellular energy)
  • May enhance response to annual flu shot and other vaccines — a single dose on vaccination day is an easy win
  • Non-hormonal — no interaction with HRT or hormonal protocols
  • Avoid during pregnancy and breastfeeding — safety data insufficient

Women's Considerations

Women's physiology differs from men's in ways that matter significantly for peptide use. Hormonal cycles, body composition differences, life phases, and sensitivity levels all affect how peptides should be dosed and timed.

Hormonal Cycle & Timing

Women's hormones fluctuate significantly across the month. Estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone all affect how growth hormone peptides work and how well tissue responds to repair signals. Some practitioners adjust peptide dosing around cycle phases — though the evidence base here is emerging rather than established. What is clear: peptide sensitivity can vary week to week.

💡 Track your cycle when starting a new peptide. Note energy, water retention, and side effects relative to cycle phase. This data helps you and your doctor optimize.
Perimenopause & Menopause

This is where peptides — particularly GH secretagogues — offer their most compelling case for women. GH declines with age and accelerates through perimenopause. The resulting changes (body fat redistribution, muscle loss, sleep disruption, skin changes) can be partially addressed with GH peptide protocols. Many practitioners view these as complementary to (not replacing) hormone optimization.

💡 If considering peptides alongside HRT (estrogen, progesterone, testosterone), work with a physician who understands both. The combination can be synergistic — but dose adjustments may be needed over time.
Sensitivity Differences

Women are generally more sensitive to GH-axis peptides than men. This is largely a good thing — but it also means that doses which are well-tolerated in men can produce pronounced side effects in women. Water retention, fatigue, and numbness tend to hit women harder and at lower doses. Start at roughly 50–75% of "standard" male doses as a guiding principle.

💡 "Start low, go slow" is not timid — it's smart. Building up over 2–4 weeks gives your body time to adapt and lets you identify your personal sweet spot.
Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

This is simple: essentially no peptides should be used during pregnancy or breastfeeding outside of medical necessity. GLP-1 agonists, GH peptides, and most performance-related peptides have not been tested in pregnant women and carry unknown fetal risks. BPC-157 and TB-500 are also contraindicated. Discontinue any peptide use when planning pregnancy or immediately upon confirmation.

⚠️ Inform all your care providers about any peptides you've been using when you become pregnant. Cessation timelines matter for some compounds.
Bloodwork: What to Test

Peptide use without monitoring is flying blind. Key markers to track before starting and at regular intervals:

IGF-1 (GH peptide use) Fasting glucose & insulin Comprehensive metabolic panel Thyroid (TSH, Free T3/T4) Sex hormones (E2, Prog, Total T, SHBG) HbA1c (GLP-1 users) Lipid panel CBC
💡 Baseline labs before starting. Retest at 6–8 weeks. Quarterly if on ongoing protocols. This is non-negotiable — data protects you.

Red Flags & Safety

@jkharve consistently emphasizes that knowing when NOT to proceed is just as important as knowing how to use peptides effectively. Here are the warning signs to take seriously.

🚩 Active Cancer or High Cancer Risk
Growth hormone peptides, TB-500, and BPC-157 all have pro-angiogenic (new blood vessel formation) properties. For healthy individuals, this supports healing. For anyone with active cancer, this same mechanism could theoretically support tumor growth. Strong family history of certain cancers warrants serious discussion with an oncologist before any GH-axis peptide use. This is a hard contraindication for active cancer.
🚩 Uncontrolled Diabetes
Growth hormone is counter-regulatory to insulin — meaning it raises blood glucose. GH peptides in someone with poorly controlled diabetes can significantly worsen glycemic control. GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are actually used to treat diabetes, but they require careful medication adjustments when combined with existing diabetes drugs to avoid hypoglycemia.
🚩 Cardiovascular Disease
PT-141 transiently increases blood pressure — potentially dangerous in those with hypertension or cardiovascular disease. GH peptides can also affect cardiac function. Anyone with existing cardiovascular conditions needs physician clearance and ongoing monitoring before any peptide protocol.
🚩 No Medical Supervision
Self-prescribing peptides from online research without physician oversight is genuinely risky. Lab work can catch problems you can't feel. Dosing errors compound over time. And if something goes wrong, you need a provider who knows your full picture. This is non-negotiable safety infrastructure, not bureaucracy.
🚩 Unverified / Gray-Market Sources
The peptide market has significant quality variation. Compounds sold as "research chemicals" may contain incorrect concentrations, impurities, or entirely wrong compounds. Peptides sourced outside pharmacy channels should at minimum have Certificate of Analysis (COA) from an independent third-party lab. The cheapest option is rarely safe.
🚩 Stacking Too Much Too Soon
Adding multiple peptides at once makes it impossible to identify what's causing a side effect. Start with one compound, run it for 4–6 weeks, understand your response, then consider additions. This is how professionals build protocols — iteratively, with data.
🚩 Ignoring Side Effects as "Normal"
Some initial side effects (mild water retention on GH peptides, transient nausea on GLP-1s) are expected and resolve. Others — persistent elevated heart rate, significant glucose changes, severe GI distress, numbness that doesn't resolve — need attention. "Powering through" side effects is not a strategy; it's a risk.

Sourcing & Quality

One of the most critical — and most often skipped — parts of any peptide discussion. Where you source compounds matters as much as what you choose to use.

Prescription vs. Research Chemical
Some peptides (semaglutide, tirzepatide, sermorelin, PT-141/Vyleesi) are FDA-approved and available by prescription. These undergo rigorous quality control. Others exist in a gray market as "research chemicals" — technically sold for laboratory research, but widely used by humans. Prescription is always preferable where available. For research-grade compounds, third-party testing (COA) is essential.
Compounding Pharmacies
Licensed compounding pharmacies can legally produce peptides like semaglutide, CJC-1295/Ipamorelin, sermorelin, and BPC-157 under physician prescription. This is the gold standard for research-grade peptides used in human protocols. These pharmacies operate under FDA oversight and produce compounds to specific quality standards. Your prescribing physician should be directing you to specific compounding partners they trust.
What to Look For: COA (Certificate of Analysis)
Any legitimate peptide source should provide a COA from an independent third-party analytical lab. This document confirms: compound identity (is it actually what they claim?), purity percentage (high-quality peptides are typically 98%+), absence of contaminants or endotoxins (especially important for injectables). Never inject a peptide without a COA. Endotoxin contamination can cause severe inflammatory reactions.
Red Flags in Sourcing
Be cautious of: extremely low prices (quality peptide synthesis is not cheap), no third-party COA available, vague sourcing information, shipping from unknown overseas facilities without US or EU pharmaceutical compliance, websites that market directly to human use while claiming "research only" status. If a source feels sketchy, it probably is.
Reconstitution & Storage
Most injectable peptides come as lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder that must be reconstituted with bacteriostatic water. This requires sterile technique. Reconstituted peptides should be stored refrigerated (not frozen) and used within 2–4 weeks typically. Always use appropriate insulin syringes (typically 1mL, 29–31 gauge). Improper reconstitution or storage degrades the peptide and wastes your investment — or worse, creates contamination risk.

Glossary

The vocabulary of peptide use can be dense. Here are the terms you'll encounter most often, explained plainly.

Peptide
A short chain of amino acids (the building blocks of proteins). Peptides act as signaling molecules in the body — telling cells what to do. The difference between a peptide and a protein is size: peptides are smaller.
Subcutaneous (SubQ)
Injection into the fatty tissue just under the skin, not into muscle. Most peptides are administered this way using a short, fine insulin needle. Belly, love handles, or thigh are common sites.
GH (Growth Hormone)
A hormone produced by the pituitary gland that influences body composition, metabolism, and recovery. It declines with age. Many peptides work by stimulating your body's own GH production rather than introducing exogenous GH.
IGF-1
Insulin-like Growth Factor 1. Produced in the liver in response to GH. IGF-1 is the main downstream mediator of GH's anabolic (tissue-building) effects. Blood testing for IGF-1 tells you whether GH peptides are working.
GHRH
Growth Hormone Releasing Hormone. A signal from the hypothalamus that tells the pituitary to release GH. CJC-1295 and Sermorelin are GHRH analogues — they mimic this natural signal.
GHRP
Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide. Works through a different receptor (ghrelin receptor) to stimulate GH release. Ipamorelin is a GHRP. Combining GHRH + GHRP creates a synergistic pulse effect.
GLP-1
Glucagon-Like Peptide-1. A gut hormone released after eating that slows digestion, signals fullness, and regulates blood sugar. Semaglutide and tirzepatide mimic this hormone to produce their effects.
Lyophilized
Freeze-dried. Most research-grade peptides come in this powder form because it's stable at room temperature. Before injection, the powder must be reconstituted (dissolved) with bacteriostatic water.
Bacteriostatic Water
Sterile water with a small amount of benzyl alcohol added to prevent bacterial growth. Used to reconstitute lyophilized peptides. Regular sterile water is not appropriate because it doesn't inhibit bacteria growth in the vial over time.
Half-Life
How long a compound stays active in your body at 50% of its original concentration. Short half-life (like Sermorelin, ~minutes) means timing is critical. Longer half-life (like CJC-1295 with DAC, days) allows more flexible dosing.
Angiogenesis
The formation of new blood vessels. BPC-157 and TB-500 both promote angiogenesis — in healthy tissue, this supports healing. The concern with cancer: tumors also require blood vessel formation to grow.
Loading Phase
An initial period of higher dosing designed to saturate receptors or achieve therapeutic tissue levels quickly. TB-500 loading (higher doses for 4–6 weeks) followed by a lower maintenance dose is a classic example.
COA (Certificate of Analysis)
A document from a third-party laboratory confirming what a compound is, how pure it is, and that it's free of contaminants. Absolutely necessary before injecting any research-grade peptide.
Telomeres
Protective caps on the ends of chromosomes, like the plastic tips of shoelaces. They shorten with each cell division, and shorter telomeres are associated with cellular aging. Epithalon is thought to activate telomerase, the enzyme that lengthens them.
Titration
Gradually increasing a dose over time to find the minimum effective dose with the fewest side effects. GLP-1 medications always start at low doses and are titrated up monthly. Smart peptide protocols do the same.
Bioavailability
The percentage of a compound that actually reaches systemic circulation to have an effect. Oral peptide bioavailability is typically very low (digestive enzymes break them down) — which is why most are injected. Exception: oral semaglutide is specially formulated to improve absorption.
