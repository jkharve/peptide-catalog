Section 01

What is Tirzepatide?

Tirzepatide is a molecule that simultaneously binds to both GLP-1 receptors and GIP receptors throughout your body. This dual action is what sets it apart from semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy), which only targets GLP-1 receptors — and why tirzepatide tends to produce more significant weight loss results.

It's prescribed as Mounjaro for type 2 diabetes management and Zepbound for weight loss. Both are the same molecule at the same doses — just different FDA-approved indications.

GLP-1 Receptor Effects
  • Pancreas releases more insulin in response to food
  • Stomach empties ~36 minutes slower on average
  • Small intestine takes hours longer to empty
  • Brain signals that contribute to feeling full
  • May slow neural degeneration (emerging research)
  • Also responsible for nausea side effects
GIP Receptor Effects (Tirzepatide only)
  • Releases additional insulin + improves insulin sensitivity
  • Actively inhibits the GLP-1-induced nausea pathway
  • Contributes to greater fat loss vs GLP-1 alone
  • May improve adipose tissue metabolism
vs Semaglutide
Key Difference

Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) activates GLP-1 receptors only. Tirzepatide activates both GLP-1 and GIP — the GIP component adds insulin sensitivity benefits and, importantly, partially counteracts GLP-1-induced nausea. This is one reason many people tolerate tirzepatide's side effects better than expected.

Section 02

Dosing & Titration

Tirzepatide is injected once per week into a fat deposit. The protocol starts low and moves up gradually — this is called titration, and it's critical to allow your body to adjust and to find your personal optimal dose. Don't rush it.

⏱

Half-Life: 5 Days

Because tirzepatide has a 5-day half-life, it accumulates in your body above the dose you inject. For example, after 4 weekly injections of 2.5 mg, you'll actually peak at about ~3 mg in your system. Important Notes on Titration
  • Standard protocol: start 2.5 mg × 4 weeks, then decide whether to increase by 2.5 mg each step
  • You do not have to reach a higher dose — some people achieve full results at 2.5–5 mg
  • It's also typical to titrate down to a lower dose before stopping entirely — don't stop abruptly
  • Average weight loss is 0.5–2 pounds per week — this is a long-term project, not a sprint
  • Healthy weight loss is generally no more than 1% of body weight per week
Section 03

Should I Increase My Dose?

This framework is specifically for people using tirzepatide for weight loss. It's a useful starting point — but always make these decisions with your prescribing physician, not unilaterally.

Ask yourself all four questions:

1
Do I still have weight to lose?
If you've reached your goal, there may be no reason to increase — maintenance protocols exist.
2
Have I taken at least 4 shots at this dose?
Your body needs time to reach steady state at a new dose before you can accurately assess its effect. Don't judge a dose before 4 weeks.
3
Are my side effects manageable?
Increasing dose while already struggling with nausea, fatigue, or GI symptoms will usually make things worse. Stabilize first.
4
Have I lost less than 2 lbs over the past 2 weeks?
(Or less than 1% of body weight over 2 weeks?) If weight loss has stalled, a dose increase may help restart progress.
✅ All four yes → consider increasing
Section 04

How to Inject

Tirzepatide is injected subcutaneously — just under the skin, into a fat deposit. It's a small needle and most people find it straightforward after the first few times.

🧴
Keep it sterile Wipe the top of the vial with an alcohol pad before drawing. Never store medication in a syringe between uses. Never reuse a syringe.
💉
Two vials, two syringes If you need to draw from two separate vials to get your dose, use two separate syringes — one per vial.
🌬️
Breathe through it Breathe in, then inject as you breathe out. This simple technique significantly reduces the sensation of the injection.
📐
Double-check your dose Compounded tirzepatide especially — concentration, volume, units, and dose are easy to confuse. Use a dosing calculator at fatscientist.com.

Section 05

Storage & Travel

At Home

Store pens and vials in the refrigerator. Do not store syringes in the fridge — condensation can contaminate them. Keep at room temperature, away from light.

Flying / Travel

Pack medication in your carry-on — never checked luggage. Bring your prescription. Travel cases with ice packs are available for full vials. Pre-drawn syringes are stable at room temperature for 21 days (keep below 86°F / 30°C).

Temperature

Never freeze. Never leave in a hot car. Once opened or reconstituted (compounded), check your pharmacy's specific guidelines — typically 28–30 days refrigerated.

Syringes

Dispose of used syringes in a sharps container. Never throw loose needles in the trash. Many pharmacies offer sharps disposal.

Section 06

Hydration & Electrolytes

Tirzepatide acts as a diuretic for most people. Combined with eating less food (and therefore less sodium), many people quickly enter a cycle where drinking plain water is actually making dehydration worse — because you need water in balance with sodium, potassium, and magnesium.

Signs You're Electrolyte Deficient
  • Muscle cramps — especially legs and feet
  • Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep
  • Headaches that don't respond to water alone
  • Brain fog or difficulty concentrating
  • Heart palpitations (seek medical attention if severe)
Solutions That Work
🥥 Coconut water 🍖 Bone broth 🍋 Lemon water + salt ⚡ Electrolyte supplements (LMNT, Liquid IV, etc.)

Stock these before you start, not after symptoms appear. Many people wish they had done this from day one.

Section 07

Protecting Muscle Mass

This is arguably the most important section. All significant caloric restriction causes some muscle loss — tirzepatide is no different, and because appetite suppression can be aggressive, the risk is real. Muscle loss is invisible on the scale (it looks like weight loss) but harmful to your metabolism long-term.

What Actually Works
  • Protein: Target 1g per pound of lean body mass daily — non-negotiable. When you're not hungry, protein shakes become essential tools, not optional
  • Strength training: 3–4x per week minimum. Resistance training is the most powerful signal to your body to preserve muscle while in a caloric deficit
  • Adequate calories: Extreme restriction accelerates muscle loss. Aim for a moderate deficit, not starvation
  • Track protein obsessively: Not forever — but while adapting to the medication, tracking ensures you're hitting your target on low-appetite days
Hair Loss Note

Hair loss can occur as a side effect of rapid weight loss — it's called telogen effluvium and is a physiological response to caloric stress, not the medication itself. Adequate protein intake is the primary lever within your control to minimize it. It is typically temporary and resolves as weight loss slows.

Section 08

Common Questions

What if I miss a dose?
If it's within 4 days of your missed dose, take it as soon as you remember and restart your weekly schedule from that day. If more than 4 days have passed, skip it and wait for your next scheduled dose. Never double-dose.
How long until I see results?
Most people notice appetite changes within the first 1–2 weeks. Meaningful weight loss typically becomes visible over 4–12 weeks. The medication works best as a long-term protocol — average loss is 0.5–2 lbs per week, with total weight loss in trials averaging 15–22% of body weight over 72 weeks.
Can I drink alcohol?
Alcohol is not contraindicated, but tirzepatide slows gastric emptying which can intensify alcohol's effects. Many people find their alcohol tolerance changes significantly. Use extra caution, especially early in treatment.
What's the difference between brand-name and compounded?
Brand-name (Mounjaro/Zepbound) is manufactured to strict FDA standards as pre-filled pens. Compounded tirzepatide is mixed at a pharmacy, typically comes in vials, and requires you to draw your own dose. Compounded is often significantly cheaper but requires careful attention to concentration and dosing calculations — always use a dosing calculator.
What happens when I stop taking it?
Weight regain is common if no lifestyle changes have been established during treatment. This is not a personal failure — it reflects the underlying biology of obesity and appetite regulation. The most successful outcomes come from using the medication to build sustainable habits around food, protein, and movement that persist after discontinuation. Always titrate down gradually rather than stopping abruptly.
Can I use this alongside other peptides?
Some practitioners combine tirzepatide with CJC-1295/Ipamorelin to offset muscle loss, and BPC-157 for joint support as new physical activity increases. Always discuss any combinations with your prescribing physician — drug interactions and overlapping metabolic effects need to be managed carefully.
